# Interior Design: People Worth Knowing

*A reference for Olivia. Two lists, on purpose.*

**The canon** is a compass: foundations that will still be true in forty years. Don't read it all at once; return to a name when a unit makes it relevant.

**Current practice** is a snapshot of today (June 2026). Studios open, close and merge, so the last section is how you keep it alive yourself.

---

## Part 1: The Canon (foundations that don't date)

### Design philosophy and spatial thinking

- **Christopher Alexander.** Built his ideas by watching how people actually use rooms, then turned it into rules you can test against any plan. Start with *A Pattern Language*, dipped into. Connects to Space and Scale (VOC02): his patterns tell you *why* a layout feels alive or dead.
- **Francis D.K. Ching.** Turned the invisible grammar of design (scale, proportion, ordering) into drawings a beginner can see. Start with *Interior Design Illustrated*. It sits directly under Design Foundations (VOC01). When a scheme looks "off," the answer is usually a Ching principle.

### The craft of execution

- **Peter Zumthor.** Obsesses over what a space does to a body: light, material, temperature, sound. Start with *Atmospheres* (short). Pair with Tanizaki's essay *In Praise of Shadows*. This is the half of lighting and Interior Schemes (VOC02, VOC03) no render teaches.
- **Ilse Crawford.** Founding editor of Elle Decoration UK who became the clearest voice for interiors as a human discipline: a room is judged by how it makes you feel and how it works, not how it photographs. Interiors-native, not borrowed from architecture. Start with her episode of *Abstract: The Art of Design* (Netflix, short), then study Ett Hem in Stockholm. Connects to Interior Schemes (VOC03), and she is the direct antidote to the first trap below: design for the body and the senses first, let the styling come last.
- **Florence Knoll.** More or less invented the disciplined method of modern interior design: the scaled material board, the total scheme, run as a profession not a hobby. Look up her "paste-ups." That is exactly the skill Interior Schemes (VOC03) is drilling.

### Residential interiors and the real conditions of working in Australia

- **Robin Boyd.** Looked hard at the Australian home and named its faults from first principles. Read *The Australian Ugliness* (1960). Sharpens how you read a brief and a site here.
- **Marion Hall Best (1905 to 1988).** Sydney's pre-eminent 20th-century interior designer and a founding member of the Society of Interior Designers of Australia in 1951; she fought to have interior design recognised as a profession at all. Argued the intense Australian light demands intense colour. See the Marion Hall Best Collection (Museums of History NSW), and the book *The Best Style*. The field you're training into was built here, by a woman, who started in Dubbo.
- **The honest part:** the real operating knowledge of Australian practice is not in any author. It lives in the National Construction Code and the Australian Standards, in the trades (a good cabinetmaker knows what holds and what fails), and in studio mentorship. Your construction and joinery units (VOC05, VOC10) and your first job are where you get it. Carry Glenn Murcutt's "touch this earth lightly" into all of it: design to the site, light and climate first.

### Renovation and adaptive reuse

- **Carlo Scarpa.** The acknowledged master of the junction where new work meets old fabric, proven through buildable detailing, not theory. Look at the Castelvecchio museum, Verona. This is Joinery Detailing (VOC10) at its highest level. Most Australian residential work is renovation, so this is bread and butter.
- **Lina Bo Bardi.** Took tired buildings and gave them vivid new public life, centred on how people actually gather. See SESC Pompeia, Sao Paulo (a factory turned cultural centre). Connects to Commercial and Hospitality (VOC09, VOC11).

## Part 2: Current Australian Practice (snapshot, June 2026)

### Residential studios in Sydney doing rigorous work

- **Arent&Pyke.** Plan and materiality do the work before the palette does. Study a house plan and its circulation first, ignore the styling.
- **Hare + Klein.** Decades-old Sydney studio built on tactile materials and interiors made to age well. Study how they layer texture across one room.
- **Richards Stanisich.** Two registered architects (ex-SJB Interiors Sydney), hands-on with builders, strong on heritage meeting contemporary. Study how new work meets old fabric: Scarpa, built in Sydney.

### Commercial, retail and hospitality (where most junior work sits)

- **Akin Atelier (Kelvin Ho).** Architects, designers and strategists; real material innovation (the Art Gallery of NSW resin gallery shop). Study how a hospitality fit-out survives heavy use and still controls light. Large enough to hire.
- **George Livissianis.** Restrained, material-led Sydney hospitality. Study how much he achieves by removing things, not adding them.
- **SJB.** Multidisciplinary, founded 1970; documentation and build rigour at scale (e.g. The Eve Hotel, Sydney). A place that teaches how a real construction set comes together.
- *(Also worth following, though national rather than Sydney: Pattern Studio for retail, Acme for hospitality.)*

### Practices that actually train juniors and graduates

This matters most early on. The famous boutique and the studio that gives a diploma grad a desk are usually not the same place. Right now, reachability beats prestige.

- The big multidisciplinary firms (**SJB, DKO, Woods Bagot, Bates Smart, Cox**) run structured junior and graduate intake and teach documentation properly. Year one drafting inside one of these beats styling moodboards in a glamorous two-person studio. The prestige studio is a year-three move.
- **GradSpace (gradspace.com.au)** is the single most useful channel for you: a free board that exists to connect Sydney Design School to industry. The studios posting there have already decided to hire from your exact qualification. Most students never use it properly. You should.

## How to keep this list current

The names above date; these sources don't.

- **Australian Interior Design Awards.** The benchmark, judged entirely by peers (2026 winners announced 12 June at the Sofitel Wentworth, Sydney). Reading the shortlist and jury comments each year is a free masterclass in what the profession rates as substantive.
- **Design Institute of Australia (DIA).** The professional body. Take student membership; go to the talks; meet practising designers early.
- **Publications:** ArchitectureAU, est living, Indesignlive, Habitus.
- **Job boards:** GradSpace first, then SEEK, Jora, Adzuna. Filter for "junior," "graduate," "interior design assistant," not "interior designer" (which usually wants 3+ years).

## The two traps

1. **The high-follower stylist with no buildable documentation.** Tell: a portfolio of styled hero shots, zero plans, sections or details, all "vibe" and no spec. Fine to follow for presentation ideas. A poor model for what the job actually is.
2. **The studio that uses interns as unpaid moodboard labour.** Tell: a vague, unpaid "internship" with no mention of mentorship or what you'll produce. In Australia this can also breach Fair Work: ongoing unpaid work that is really a job is often unlawful. The question that flushes it out: *"What will I be doing in my first week, and who reviews my drawings?"* A good answer involves drawings and a named person. A bad one involves Pinterest and the coffee machine.
