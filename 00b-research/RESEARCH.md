## Strand 1: Client and event

**Client / event:** Sydney Festival, the city's flagship summer arts festival,
running roughly three weeks each January. It programs around 800 local and
international artists across ~50 venues and draws in the order of 450,000 visitors
a year. Remit is broad and deliberately inclusive: a mix of free and ticketed
work, accessible pricing, and a spread from large spectacle to intimate
participatory pieces.

**Carriageworks is a confirmed Festival venue.** The 2026 program puts three works
in the building over 14-25 January (WAKE, BURGERZ, and Conflictorium, the last an
interactive "museum of conflict" in Bay 19). So a Class 6 food/beverage pop-up sits
inside a precinct already pulling festival crowds for performance and participatory
art, with foot traffic across day and evening.

**What this means for the brief:** the client is not a single café operator, it is
the Festival as host. The pop-up has to earn its place in a curated arts program,
not just trade. That favours a concept that reads as an *experience* (sensory,
interactive) as much as a place to buy a drink, which lines up with the two-zone
requirement.

**Open question:** is the "client" the Festival itself, or a named food/beverage
operator the student invents to trade under the Festival banner? The base brief
template asks for a client; confirm which framing the educator wants.

## Strand 2: Site (Carriageworks)

- Former **Eveleigh Carriage Workshops**, Redfern. A long 19th-century brick shed
  built 1880-1889, closed 1988, reopened as an arts precinct in 2007.
- Heritage character: **repetitive iron-framed gabled bays**, exposed brick, raw
  industrial patina, retained machinery and artefacts.
- **Tonkin Zulaikha Greer** adaptive reuse (AIA Architecture Award 2008, plus a
  heritage Greenway Award). The move that defines it: new program is inserted as
  **discrete concrete boxes clearly articulated from the heritage fabric**, using
  the building's structural grid as the ordering device. New work is legible as
  new; the old fabric keeps its age.
- Bays are referenced by number (e.g. Bay 19). The build is a series of repeating
  structural bays under gabled iron trusses.

**Design lesson to carry forward (Scarpa principle from the base reference):** where
new work meets old fabric, make the join honest and legible. Don't pastiche the
heritage; insert something clearly contemporary that respects the grid. That is
literally what TZG did, and an assessor at this site will look for it.

**Open question / honest-dimensions flag:** I do **not** have a confirmed measured
bay size (span x depth x ridge height). The rendered plan is assessed on being
*measured and code-aware*, so before we draw it we need a real bay dimension. Best
source is the Carriageworks venue-hire / technical spec pack, or the educator's
supplied site plan. Do not eyeball it.

**Update (2026-06-03):** Carriageworks Bay 20 published dimensions are 33 x 16.5m,
528m2, 6.20m truss height (official venue page; seated bank 272, standing 500 to 800).
Use as the working reference. Other bays differ (Bays 22 to 24 total over 4,200m2).
Confirm Olivia's assigned bay before finalising the plan.

**Footprint (2026-06-03):** CURA's design area is **80m2** within a bay (roughly 8 x 10m),
open to the soaring volume above. The bay sets the surrounding grid, brick and height; the
80m2 is the leasable pop-up footprint the measured plan is drawn to. Tight for two zones plus
the dye-studio wet area, so zoning and circulation must be resolved early.

## Strand 3: The six designers (research all six, then choose one)

The brief is to research all six, then champion **one** as the influence the concept
traces back to. Short synthesis of each and what it would give an immersive,
sensory, sustainable F&B pop-up in a heritage bay:

1. **Kazuyo Sejima (SANAA)** - lightness, transparency, non-hierarchical plans,
   glass and thin steel, interior/exterior dissolved. *Gives:* an airy, permeable
   insertion that floats inside the heavy brick bay (strong contrast play). *Tension:*
   lots of glass reads less "sustainable / low-impact, demountable" unless handled
   carefully.

2. **Debris Facility Pty Ltd** - Melbourne art practice working through
   "parasitism", transmutation of industrial waste materials, supply-chain critique,
   wearables and installation. *Gives:* a genuinely radical sustainability narrative
   (reuse, post-commodity materials) and conceptual edge. *Tension:* more art-provocation
   than hospitality; harder to land a commercial, code-compliant, comfortable F&B space.

3. **Janet Laurence** (the "Lawrence" in the brief) - Sydney environmental artist,
   35+ years, immersive environments from ash, minerals, living plants, glass,
   veils and specimens; themes of nature's fragility, healing, alchemy, perception.
   *Gives:* a ready-made multi-sensory and sustainability spine (plants = smell,
   sight, touch, humidity, even taste via botanicals), and she's local. *Strong fit*
   for the three-senses + sustainability requirements.

4. **Do Ho Suh** - Korean installation artist; full-scale translucent fabric
   reconstructions of homes, sewn polyester on steel; memory, migration, home.
   *Gives:* a literal soft-architecture device, a glowing fabric "room within the
   bay" you can walk through (the interactive zone almost designs itself). *Tension:*
   fabric vs Class 6 flammability index and food-prep hygiene needs careful handling.

5. **Olafur Eliasson** - Icelandic-Danish; light, colour, water, mist, mono-colour
   rooms, artificial weather (The Weather Project, Room for one colour). Explicit
   interest in the senses and dissolving the line between viewer and work. *Gives:*
   the clearest engine for a sensory and a day-vs-night / dawn-dusk shift, and a
   showpiece interactive zone. *Tension:* spectacle can outrun the design substance
   if not tied to plan and materials.

6. **Kengo Kuma** - Japanese architect; "anti-object", natural materials (timber,
   stone, bamboo, paper), traditional joinery, lightness, building in dialogue with
   site rather than dominating it. *Gives:* warm, tactile, demountable timber/bamboo
   language that sits respectfully in the heritage bay and reads sustainable;
   joinery = touch and craft. *Strong fit* for sustainability + heritage + tactility.

**My read (for discussion, not a decision):** the three that most directly serve
*minimum three senses + sustainability central + heritage-bay insertion* are
**Janet Laurence** (nature/healing, local, multi-sensory by default), **Kengo Kuma**
(natural materials, demountable, respectful insertion), and **Olafur Eliasson**
(strongest interactive/sensory showpiece and the dawn-dusk shift). Sejima is the
elegant-contrast play; Debris and Do Ho Suh are higher-concept and higher-risk
against the commercial and code constraints. **The choice is the student's.**

**Decision (2026-06-03):** Olivia champions **two**, split by zone, as the CURA
concept: **Kengo Kuma** anchors the commercial zone (the how - layered natural
materials, timber/bamboo/stone, tactile craft, demountable build) and **Janet
Laurence** anchors the interactive zone (the why - biophilia, living botanicals,
nature as care, the natural-dye personalisation studio). The base brief asks for
*one* designer, so championing two carries assessment risk; confirm the educator
accepts it. Eliasson is not used.

## Strand 4: Target customers

No fine-grained recent demographic breakdown is published; best available is
attendance scale plus an older (2011) survey. Working profile:

- Mostly Greater Sydney residents (~83% in the 2011 survey), plus interstate and
  international festival-goers.
- Broad age range by design (inclusive programming, free + ticketed), but a summer
  arts festival skews toward culturally engaged adults, couples and friend groups,
  families at daytime, and a younger evening crowd around performance.
- At Carriageworks specifically: people already there for performance / participatory
  art (WAKE, BURGERZ, Conflictorium), so the customer arrives primed for an
  *experience*, often with time to linger before or after a show.

**For the concept:** design for two moments, a daytime browse/refuel and an evening
pre/post-show drink, which supports the day-vs-night lighting scheme the schedule
needs, and the commercial + interactive zone split (buy something, then experience
something, or the reverse).
