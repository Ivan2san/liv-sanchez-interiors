## The design concept

CURA is a temporary slow-fashion and lifestyle pop-up: a space of quiet intention inside the
energy of the Festival. Care in the making, care in the choosing, care for the natural world.
Rammed-earth walls layered in warm ochres, terracotta and sandy neutrals form the bones of
the space; raw timber, stone and living botanicals bring texture and life. It should feel
ancient and considered, as if it has always belonged to the landscape, set against the raw
industrial backdrop of a Carriageworks heritage bay. The join between new work and old brick
stays honest and contemporary (the TZG and Scarpa principle), never pastiche.

## Championed influences (both, one per zone)

The decision is to champion two designers, one per zone, as equal influences. Confirm
Olivia's educator accepts two; the base SDS09 brief asks for one.

- **Kengo Kuma - the HOW (commercial zone).** The "eraser of buildings": layered natural
  materials (timber, bamboo, stone), tactile craft, structures that dissolve into their
  setting. Drives the retail edit's material and joinery language and the demountable build.
- **Janet Laurence - the WHY (interactive zone).** Australian environmental artist; biophilia,
  living botanical specimens, nature as care and healing. Drives the personalisation studio
  and the botanical installations. (Spelling: the artist is **Laurence**; Olivia's deck spells
  it "Lawrence". Fix it, an assessor will check the name.)

## Two zones (required)

- **Commercial zone - the retail edit (Kuma):** a curated edit of slow-fashion garments and
  lifestyle objects on raw-timber shelving and stone plinths; day trade.
- **Interactive zone - the personalisation studio (Laurence):** a hands-on workshop where
  visitors customise a natural-linen piece with botanical printing and natural dyes to take
  home, set among living and dried botanical installations.

## Senses engaged (four, exceeds the minimum three) - per the deck

- **Sight:** layered rammed-earth tones, ochre and terracotta, raw-timber shelving, filtered
  natural light, living botanical installations, curated product displays.
- **Touch:** rammed-earth walls (raw layered texture), smooth stone plinths, unfinished timber
  benches, soft raw-linen garments. Tactility is central to the slow experience.
- **Smell:** honest natural scent only - raw earth, unfinished timber, dried botanicals, the
  subtle fragrance of natural dyes. No artificial fragrance.
- **Sound:** a low, curated ambient soundtrack, slow minimal music with gentle nature sounds,
  felt rather than noticed.

## Materials and palette

Rammed earth (layered ochre, terracotta and sand strata), raw and unfinished timber, smooth
stone, bamboo, raw and naturally dyed linen, living and dried botanicals. All matte, natural
and untreated. Set against raw Carriageworks brick and iron-framed gabled structure.

## Sustainability (central, assessed)

The elemental palette carries the argument. Each material's credential gets keyed in the
sample-board legend later: rammed earth (natural, low embodied energy, reusable as blocks),
reclaimed or FSC timber (renewable), stone (natural, reusable), bamboo (rapidly renewable),
raw linen (natural fibre, biodegradable), natural dyes (non-toxic, biodegradable), living
botanicals (replantable or compostable). The whole insertion is demountable for a three-week
pop-up.

## NCC and code constraints (capture now, resolve in the plan)

- **Class 6** (shop / retail and the supply of services direct to the public; covers both the
  retail edit and the personalisation studio).
- **Egress:** clear paths and exits; the studio needs a defined entry and exit.
- **Accessibility:** accessible circulation, an accessible retail counter and an accessible
  workshop bench.
- **Balustrades:** 1m minimum at any level change or edge.
- **Flammability index:** raw-linen drapes, the bamboo screen and timber linings must meet the
  flammability requirement inside the heritage shed.
- **Personalisation / dye studio (this replaces the old food-prep requirement):** treat as a
  wet area - sink and waste-water disposal, ventilation, slip-resistant floor, and safe
  handling and storage of dyes and mordants. If botanical printing uses steam or heat, manage
  it.
- **Rammed-earth buildability flag:** monolithic in-situ rammed earth is very heavy and not
  demountable. For a three-week pop-up, resolve it as prefabricated or modular rammed-earth
  blocks/panels (reusable) or a lightweight rammed-earth-faced system, and check floor loading
  on the heritage slab. Do not hand-wave this; the substance check and the assessor will look
  for it.

**Footprint constraint: 80m2** (confirmed by Ivan, 2026-06-03). CURA occupies a defined ~80m2

Working bay reference from the official Carriageworks venue page: **Bay 20 is 33 x 16.5m,

**Planning implication:** 80m2 is tight for two zones plus a wet-area dye studio. Expect the
retail edit, the personalisation studio (with sink, bench and storage), accessible circulation
(0.8 to 1.0m), and egress to compete for area. Zoning and a clear customer path
(browse then make) need resolving early, not at render stage.

## Target customers (per the deck)

Visitors happy to spend on quality and experiences; young families and 30 to 50s primarily;
social-media savvy; art and festival goers; couples, friends and solo visitors; interested in
wellness and natural living; sustainability conscious; plus tourists. Design for
browse-then-make: browse the edit, then make something in the studio.

heritage fabric. Keep the dye-studio services and the rammed-earth buildability resolved, not
